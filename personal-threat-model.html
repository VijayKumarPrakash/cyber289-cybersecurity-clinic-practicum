Personal Threat Model

Vijay Kumar Prakash

Who Am I?

From My Perspective

A handsome, friendly guy trying to finish his capstone project, land a job, and maybe win a chess game for once.

From an Attacker's Perspective

Scrawny, brown Berkeley kid (so probably smart) who presumably carries decent electronics. Unlikely to involve police, not physically intimidating, limited local support network. Either a soft target or not worth the effort.

What I Want to Protect

Safety Autonomy Reputation Livelihood Relationships

Contextual Factors

Since I am an international student and don't want trouble with immigration or law enforcement, I am less likely to report incidents‚ÄîI don't want my name on police reports, even as the complainant. In my first week at Berkeley, I lost my Clipper card. I could have replaced it for free with a police report, but I paid the $25 fine instead.

Consequences of Failure

I lose some money, but not a whole lot

A bad credit score that follows me for life if someone opens a fraudulent account

Shame, embarrassment, or psychological repercussions if my physical safety is harmed

Comparative Perspective

I understand that different people with different backgrounds and experiences have distinct threat models.

For instance, a female classmate walking the same route at night faces a different threat calculus than I do. An undocumented student might avoid filing that police report not out of preference, but necessity.

My threat model is shaped by being a student with legal status and relatively low-value assets, but others navigating the same campus have meaningfully different concerns.
